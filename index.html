Our planets

What/When is something considered a planet?

The most recent definition of what is considered a planet, that was approved by the international astronomical union in 2006, described three things that a planet must do: 1. It must orbit a star (in our solar system we have the sun) 2. It must be big enough to have enough gravity to force it into a spherical shape 3. It must be big enough that its gravity cleared any other objects of similar size near its orbit of the sun.

Because of new observations all the time definitions may change over time. In the early 1990s astronomers found a numerous amount of ‚ÄòIcy worlds‚Äô that were orbiting the sun in a doughnut-shaped region, The Kuiper Belt, beyond Neptune‚Äôs orbit. The discovery of the Kuiper belt + its thousands of ice bodies(called KBO, Kuiper Belt Objects, another name is Transneptunians). It was suggested that Pluto was the biggest KBO instead of a planet. The IAU(international astronomical union) settled that planets and other celestial bodies(except satellites) can be divided into three categories: 1. A planet, a celestial body that; is in an orbit around a star/the sun, has sufficient mass to become a nearly round shape, and has cleared the area around its orbit 2. A dwarf planet, a celestial body that; is in an orbit around a star/the sun, has enough mass to become a nearly round shape, has not cleared the area around its orbit, and is not a satellite 3. All other objects, except satellites, that are orbiting the sun are called small solar system bodies.

Other celestial bodies

The sun and moon

the Moon

moons, they are described as celestial bodies that is kept in a planet‚Äôs gravitational pull, or a planet‚Äô natural satellite. Earth's only moon, the moon, was formed after a collision with another body, that was about the size of mars, a few billion years ago.

Moon phases refer to the different appearances of the Moon as viewed from Earth during its monthly orbit around our planet. The Moon goes through a cycle of distinct phases due to the changing positions of the Sun, Earth, and Moon. The order of the moon phases is: New moon, Waxing crescent, First quarter/Waxing half moon, Waxing gibbous, Full moon, Waning gibbous, Third quarter/Waning half moon, and finally Waning crescent. After the moon has completed her cycle in 28 days, the cycle starts again. These moon phases also make things like low and high tide possible as the gravitational pull is stronger/weaker depending where the moon is.

The moon was called the moon because we did not know that there were more moons until Galileo Gallilei discovered four more moons around jupiter in 1610. Up to this day there are a total of 751 moons discovered in our solar system, 289 of them are ‚Äòtraditional‚Äô moons and the remaining 462 are Small Body Moons (Asteroids, Kupier Belt & Trans-Neptunian Objects). Moons come in several different shapes, sizes, and formation. They can have their own atmosphere, and can also have geological features like volcanoes. Because there are so many moons being discovered it is becoming harder for astronomers to draw a line between what a moon is, more specifically, what size it needs to be to be classified as a moon.

the Sun

The Sun is the centre of our solar system. It‚Äôs an almost perfect ball of hot plasma, 5498.9 degrees celsius on the surface. The sun‚Äôs radius is about 695,000 kilometres, which is 109 times the earth‚Äôs. Its mass is 1.989 √ó 10^30 kg, this makes up for 99.86% of the total mass of the whole solar system. The Sun formed around 4.6 billion years ago from a gravitational collapse. This happens when the contraction of an astronomical object because of its own gravity, which draws matter in/towards the centre of gravity. Most of this matter went to the centre, which eventually formed the sun, the rest flattened and made the orbiting disks of the solar system. The Sun is made mostly of hydrogen gas, these atoms fuse together in the core of the sun with massive temperatures and high pressure, this process is called nuclear fusion.

The Sun is the most important source of energy for Earth. It provides light, heat, and energy for organisms on Earth, however, we do have to protect ourselves from too much ultraviolet radiation which can be harmful to our skin and eyes.
